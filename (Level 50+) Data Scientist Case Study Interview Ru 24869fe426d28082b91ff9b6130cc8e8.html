üî¶ Behaviours and signals

These behaviours are foundational for the Level 50 role and should be assessed throughout the interview, rather than in a separate section of the rubric. We‚Äôre looking for candidates who:

  • Communicate effectively: are they able to translate technical solutions for a non-technical audiences, are their responses concise.
  • Collaborates: does the candidate engages in a back-and-forth discussion, can they build upon your suggestions as the interviewer and adapt their solution.
  • Considers integration: is the candidate thinking about the business context of their solutions, and the wider teams and stakeholders that could be involved.
  • Shares expertise generously: does the candidate explain their methodology, leading you on the journey around their thought process or diving into more technical aspects.
  • Provides & seeks feedback: does the candidate actively ask for feedback? are they receptive or dismissive to new ideas?
  • Impactful contributions: are the candidate solutions clear, high-quality, and link back to impactful business value.

At Level 50 we are looking for broader signals (scope) in addition to what is outlined in the L40 rubric. We are looking for candidates who demonstrate ability in the following areas

  • Problem framing and strategic alignment: can the candidate deconstruct ambiguous business problems and connect the proposed solution directly to Monzo's wider mission and long-term goals, considering trade-offs and opportunity costs.
  • Technical authority & solution design: does the candidate design (and lead) a robust, scalable, and reusable end-to-end technical solution, weighing architectural trade-offs, managing technical debt, and influencing other technical teams.
  • Commercial impact: can the candidate explain a clear, actionable strategy that directly connects technical work to measurable business value, such as profitability or customer lifetime value, while aligning with Monzo's customer-centric values.
  • Management of delivery: can the candidate demonstrate how they deliver impact through others and drive projects forward through effective communication and stakeholder management?
    • Can the candidate explain how they work with and influence technical and non technical stakeholders to build relationship and deliver a good solution for Monzo/our customers
    • Leading and empowering a team, delegating tasks appropriately, managing resources, and balancing business needs with individual team members' growth and development.
    • Coaching and developing more junior colleagues in the case of an IC

üî¨ Running the interview

Interviewer Approach: Challenge and Redirect

At L50 we are looking for signal on multiple areas and we require higher confidence on those signals than we do for more junior roles. We also only have 1 hour with the candidate. This means interviewers must be prepared to actively intervene and guide the conversation.

  • Be a Challenging Peer: Do not let the candidate stay in presentation mode. Aim to develop a discussion to see if they can build upon your suggestions and adapt to challenge.
  • Understand Depth and Breadth:
    • If a candidate focuses on technical details, ask them to link their solution to Monzo's wider mission and consider trade-offs/opportunity cost.
      • For example, ask, "How does this align with Monzo‚Äôs strategy? What are we giving up by doing this project?".
    • If a candidate approach seems uncertain or superficial don‚Äôt be afraid to dive deep into those areas
      • For example, ask, "Why did you choose this method? What are the strengths and weaknesses of this method?".
  • Understand how they would do this in the real world: If the candidate proposes an idealised or overly complex solution (e.g., jumping straight to ML or assuming perfect data), redirect them back to the fundamentals and practical constraints. Ask, "What simple ‚ÄúA/B‚Äù test would you run first?" or "What data are you sure we have today?"
  • Look out for siloed thinking: Ask the candidate to identify key stakeholders (Product, Eng, Design) and explain how they would
    • understand different stakeholder opinions and requirements
    • communicate with / work with stakeholders to deliver the solution jointly.
  • Use the Question Bank : Have 1-2 core follow-up questions ready for each key area (Problem Framing, Commercial Impact, Delivery) to ensure you extract a medium or high confidence score in most categories

ü§ñ Interviewer Guide: Identifying AI-Generated Responses (This section is AI Generated ü§£ )

AI tools often generate highly structured, academically correct, but context-poor answers. If you are trying to confirm a response is AI, look for a combination of these features.

1. üéØ Problem Framing & Strategic Alignment

AI often excels at generating generic, multi-step problem-solving frameworks but fails to ground them in Monzo's specific context.

  • Excessive Use of Jargon/Frameworks without Customisation: The candidate spends too much time defining terms like "North Star Metric," "CRISP-DM," or "AARRR funnel" without immediately translating them into Monzo/Borderless Banking/Energy Switching terms.
    • AI Feature: Perfect structure and terminology.
    • Indicator: They propose a generic conversion metric (e.g., "sign-ups") without defining the specific point of conversion in the Monzo app for Energy Switching, or they don't explicitly link "Share of Wallet" to Monzo's commission revenue model.
  • Failure to Define Core Constraints: The candidate ignores the key trade-offs explicit in the prompt.
    • AI Feature: Provides an idealized solution.
    • Indicator (Contact Optimisation): They fail to prioritize the constraint that sending more messages risks customer fatigue/churn, making the "optimal strategy" non-trivial and requiring an explicit trade-off analysis (contact volume vs. churn risk).

2. ‚öôÔ∏è Technical Solution Design

AI models can generate textbook technical solutions that overlook practical data quality issues or focus on overly complex methods.

  • Idealised Data Sources: The candidate assumes perfect, readily available data without acknowledging the need for engineering work or proxy metrics.
    • AI Feature: Lists ideal but potentially non-existent data points.
    • Indicator (Share of Wallet): They immediately jump to complex external data sources (e.g., "bank statement scraping" or "comprehensive survey data") without proposing a solid internal proxy metric that Monzo could build using existing transaction data (e.g., total card spend vs. rent/salary deposits).
  • Over specification of ML before Foundational Work: They prematurely jump to sophisticated modeling techniques.
    • AI Feature: Recommends the latest complex ML model.
    • Indicator (Contact Optimisation): They immediately suggest an ML model (e.g., multi-armed bandit, reinforcement learning) to determine contact strategy before defining the core features, segmentation (customer lifecycle/income), and running simple A/B tests to establish a baseline Price Elasticity of Demand (PED) and Advertising Elasticity of Demand (AED).
  • Lack of Practical Test Design: They describe the concept of an A/B test but not the design.
    • Indicator: They don't specify the Minimum Detectable Effect (MDE), sample size, or the definition of the control group and treatment group in the Monzo app environment. They speak theoretically instead of practically.

3. üí∞ Commercial Impact & Measurement

AI responses often generate bland commercial metrics that don't connect to Monzo's profitability model or the strategic goals of the Borderless Banking unit.

  • Vague Commercial Link: The commercial impact is described generally ("increase revenue") instead of linking it to the core hypothesis.
    • Indicator (Share of Wallet): They focus on increasing total spend but fail to clearly articulate how this directly leads to increased commission revenue and contributes toward the goal of profitability.
    • Indicator (Contact Optimisation): They propose a metric (e.g., "conversion rate") but fail to assign a monetary value to a single energy switch conversion, making the Return on Investment (ROI) analysis impossible.
  • Ignoring Interaction Effects: They treat the metric in isolation.
    • Indicator: They focus purely on increasing adoption rate but ignore the trade-off with customer retention (churn). A strong candidate should propose an adjusted metric, like Net Value/User after a 6-month period, to balance adoption with churn.

4. ‚è≥ Project Delivery & Collaboration

AI often assumes a simple waterfall process and overlooks the need for cross-functional alignment.

  • Exclusion of Key Disciplines: The plan doesn't involve the teams necessary for execution.
    • Indicator (Contact Optimisation): The candidate forgets to involve the Marketing/Design team (for messaging content/design) and the Engineering team (for data logging/platform integration) in the core experimental design.
    • Indicator (Share of Wallet): The plan is presented as purely Data Science work, ignoring the Product team's role in designing features that drive spend or the Operations team's role in managing financial crime risk associated with increasing spend.
  • No Iterative Approach: They propose a single, perfect solution rather than an iterative roadmap.
    • Indicator: The proposal jumps immediately to the "Run" state (full optimisation/ML deployment) without defining clear checkpoints for the "Crawl" (baseline data analysis) and "Walk" (pilot $\text{A/B}$ testing) phases.

Questions to Identify AI-Generated Responses

These questions challenge the candidate's ownership of the solution, forcing them to address Monzo-specific constraints and cross-functional trade-offs that an AI model cannot easily deduce.

1. The Monzo Context Challenge (Problem Framing)

  • Question: "You proposed [Candidate's Proposed Metric, e.g., Net Value per User] for the Energy Switching case. Given Monzo‚Äôs commitment to transparency and our customer-centric values, how would you ensure this metric, which includes profit/value, is not viewed as extractive or damaging to brand trust?"
  • Probe Focus: Forces the candidate to move beyond a textbook commercial formula and align the solution with Monzo's specific ethical and strategic context. AI-generated responses struggle to integrate non-financial, customer-centric values.

2. The Data Quality Deep Dive (Technical Authority)

  • Question (Share of Wallet): "You suggested using salary and bill payments as proxies for 'Total Wallet' to solve the missing data problem. In a live Monzo environment, how would you technically define and classify a 'salary deposit' or a 'fixed bill' robustly and at scale to prevent concept drift? What specific NLP or engineering steps would be required?"
  • Probe Focus: Assesses knowledge of Monzo's internal data architecture and practical data quality challenges. AI often assumes this data is perfectly labeled. A strong candidate will discuss engineering effort, rule-based systems, or basic NLP on transaction descriptions.

3. The Guardrail and Consequence Challenge (Commercial Impact)

  • Question (Contact Optimisation): "You defined [Candidate's Guardrail Metric, e.g., unsubscribe rate] to measure customer fatigue. What is the cost of exceeding this guardrail by $10\%$? Assign a monetary value (even a proxy) to this negative outcome and explain how it influences the ROI calculation for the experiment."
  • Probe Focus: Demands a specific quantitative link between a technical/UX risk and a commercial consequence. Generic AI misses this ROI-based reasoning and usually fails to assign a monetary value to a negative interaction effect like churn/fatigue.

4. The Iterative Roadmapping Test (Project Delivery)

  • Question: "Your proposal moves quickly to a complex solution (like a Multi-Armed Bandit model). Assuming we are starting from scratch, detail the specific Go/No-Go criteria and MDE (Minimum Detectable Effect) you would set for the initial 'Crawl' $\text{A/B}$ test to prove the concept is worth moving to the 'Walk' or 'Run' phase."
  • Probe Focus: Tests the candidate's ability to plan an iterative roadmap and define statistical rigor. AI frequently jumps straight to the advanced 'Run' state. A good answer includes statistical power, sample size, and clear business thresholds.

5. The Stakeholder Alignment Test (Project Delivery & Collaboration)

  • Question (Share of Wallet): "To productionize your SOW metric, you need buy-in from the Engineering Manager. They are prioritizing latency reduction on core payments. How do you, as a Data Scientist (IC or Manager), influence them to dedicate engineering effort to the SOW metric? What is your elevator pitch to them?"
  • Probe Focus: Assesses influence without formal authority and understanding of cross-functional prioritization. AI often overlooks the political and resource trade-offs inherent in project delivery.

6. The Critique of the Model (Technical Authority)

  • Question (General): "If you ran your proposed analysis and the initial results were $\sim 10\%$ worse than expected, what are the top three most likely reasons (technical, commercial, or design-related) you would investigate first?"
  • Probe Focus: Checks for critical thinking and the ability to debug a solution across different domains (technical flaws, incorrect assumptions, commercial misalignment). AI is designed to generate a correct answer, not to preemptively critique its potential failures.
