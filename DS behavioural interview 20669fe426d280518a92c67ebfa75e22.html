1. Stakeholder management

‚è±Ô∏è15 mins

Follow-up questions

  1. What was the aim of the project?
  1. How was success defined; what part did you have in defining it?
  1. Who were the stakeholders?
  1. Where did the project complexity stem from?
  1. How did you deal with any required pushback (e.g. timelines/requirements/approach)?
  1. Looking back now, what could have been done differently?
  1. [If does not come through] How did you contribute or ensure all the stakeholders were aligned?
  1. [If does not come through] How did you communicate and/or overcome bottlenecks?
  1. [If does not come through] How did you manage competing priorities?

  • What is the intent behind the question?

    Any large project is going to experience setbacks, unforeseen changes to scope or staffing, or priorities, etc. Understanding how the candidate handled this - proactively v reactively, were an agent of defining the success and adeptly managed the communications to achieve a positive result.

    We want to know how the candidate organises themselves and manages the flow of information to other people and disciplines, ideally outside their immediate team/reporting lines. It is important to know how they considered if the thing they were doing is successful (ideally measurable) and importantly if they are able, or have reflected on the project successes & failures. An ideal candidate will have organised and executed a complex piece of work that either depended on, or was depended on by more than one other team.

2. Overcoming obstacles & course-correcting

‚è±Ô∏è10 mins

Follow-up questions

  1. What was the obstacle or barrier?
  1. What did you do?
    1. Did you have to involve others in getting to the solution?
  1. What changes took place to the project?
  1. How did you manage expectations
    1. What communication had to happen?
  1. Was it possible to have anticipated any of these obstacles or ability to be more proactive for next time?
  1. What support did you need in order to make these changes?
  1. [If does not come through] How did you manage changes to the project timeline?

  • What is the intent behind the question?

    In any meaningful project (weeks+) there will be something uncovered or an unfolding situation that you have you respond to. We‚Äôre looking to know how a candidate reacts to and manages an unforeseen event. Do they recover and can they adapt to the issue? Importantly, how do they help their stakeholders understand the impact? We also want to see the person reflect on what they learned from this issue (either being able to detect the issue in the future or how to react to it) and how they use that learning today

3. Communication with data

‚è±Ô∏è10 mins

Follow-up questions

  1. What made you feel that a change in direction was needed?
  1. What actions were you trying to persuade people to take?
  1. How were you sensitive to the other opinions/desires of the people involved?
  1. What were the tactics you used to convince people?
  1. Was your mind changed in the process of these conversations?
  1. What was the end result/decision made on this problem? How did you feel about it?

  • What is the intent behind the question?

    We‚Äôre looking for a story of influence: how this person has been able to change people‚Äôs minds. To change people‚Äôs opinion, you often have to work out what their objections are (root cause) which are frequently non-obvious. You also need to work out what matters to those people and then shape your argument to match. You may do this for example by seeking advice from others. You also need to work out what medium (presentation, spreadsheet, writing, informal chat, literature review, etc.) is going to best convey that information in the most persuasive way possible.

  • What is the intent behind the question?

    Leaving things better than you found them is a core Monzo value. Whether it‚Äôs coaching, building in learning for others, improving a methodology, bringing in repeatability and automation, improvements can come in many forms.
